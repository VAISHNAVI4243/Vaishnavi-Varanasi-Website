Customer Segmentation was all about understanding our customers better and identifying those at risk of churn. The project started with the standard RFM approach but soon added more business-specific factors like relationship length, financial discipline and quarter-on-quarter volume changes to make the model more practical. Data was taken from SAP, APIs and SQL and everything was processed in Python to calculate the scores and group customers into clear A to D categories. A Power BI dashboard was created to update results quarterly and make it easy for the business to consume. This work showed me how important cross-functional discussions, clean data flow and change management are, even when the technical part is straightforward. In the end, the segmentation helped the business take targeted actions to retain customers and reduce churn.

My initial work in the company on the "Churn Analysis" project has been expanded into a full-fledged Customer Segmentation initiative. We started with the standard industry approach, the RFM segmentation technique.

  • R stands for Recency, which is the latest date when the customer purchased from us
  • F stands for Frequency, which is how many times they purchased in that quarter
  • M stands for Monetary, which is how much value they purchased from us

We built this version quickly and presented it to the Sales Business team. But they immediately told us that the ground reality was different and segmentation needed to reflect more business-specific factors.

So we came back, ran multiple discussions with the business teams, and added a few more important parameters like:

  • Length of Relationship — how long the customer has been associated with us
  • Financial Discipline — how consistent they are with their payment terms
  • Volume Comparison — whether their purchase volumes have grown compared to the previous quarter

After reworking the model with these additional insights, we presented it again and this time the business approved it.

The initial project inception and first RFM-only iteration took one week, but all remaining iterations, validations, and final rollout collectively took around six months.

  • Even the smallest projects can take a long time when they are cross-functional
  • We had to pull data from multiple sales systems. Data extraction, SQL queries, API pulls, all of it together was time-consuming
  • Business was used to Excel reports. We created a dashboard, integrated categories into SAP, and explained to teams to ensure adoption
  • Technically simple, but change management was the key challenge

The objective was to segment existing customers into clear groups that could reveal behavior patterns and help reduce potential churn.

SAP data extraction, API based secondary data, BI warehouse for data consolidation, SQL for data extraction, Power BI for visualisation

  • Customer ID – Unique identifier for each customer
  • Date of Registration – The date the customer first registered
  • Purchase Dates – All dates on which the customer made purchases
  • Product ID – Identifier for the product purchased
  • Purchase Value – The value of each purchase
  • Payment Timeliness – Whether the customer paid fully & on time
  • Previous Quarter Purchase Value – Total purchased previously
  • Current Quarter Purchase Value – Total purchased this quarter
  1. Calculate Derived Columns: Recency, Frequency, Monetary, Length of Relationship, Financial Discipline, Value/Volume Comparison.
  2. Assign Quartile Scores: Values divided into 5 quartiles (5–1).
  3. Apply Weightages: Multiply scores & sum.
  4. Categorize: Final score grouped into A–D.

Sample Customer:

  • Recency – Purchased 10 days ago
  • Frequency – 4 purchases
  • Length – 3 years
  • Monetary – ₹5000
  • Financial Discipline – 80% on-time payments
  • Volume Comparison – 10% growth

Step 1: Assign Intra-Quartile Scores

Recency – 4
Frequency – 5
Length – 3
Monetary – 4
Financial Discipline – 4
Volume Comparison – 3

Step 2: Apply Weightages and Compute Final Score

Recency: 4 × 10% = 0.4
Frequency: 5 × 30% = 1.5
Length: 3 × 10% = 0.3
Monetary: 4 × 25% = 1.0
Financial Discipline: 4 × 15% = 0.6
Volume Comparison: 3 × 10% = 0.3
Final Score = 0.4 + 1.5 + 0.3 + 1.0 + 0.6 + 0.3 = 4.1

Step 3: Categorize

Category A – Highest quartile
Category B – Second quartile
Category C – Third quartile
Category D – Lowest quartile (highest risk of churn)

In this example, a score of 4.1 would place the customer in Category B, indicating a moderate risk of churn.

Results updated quarterly in Power BI with transition matrix.

Enabled targeted retention programs and early churn detection, enabling targeted retention and replacement strategies; increased Customer Lifetime Value by 75%, from $68K to $120K.
